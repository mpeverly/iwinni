The Western Alps are entering their summer alpine season under conditions shaped by an exceptionally heavy winter snowpack and a cold, prolonged spring. For mountain travelers planning objectives across the Mont Blanc Massif, the Bernese Oberland, and the Valais high routes, this brief synthesizes current access conditions, glacial status, and key infrastructure notes.

## Snowpack Context

The 2025–2026 winter deposited snowfall at 140–165% of the 30-year average across elevations above 2,500m from Chamonix to Zermatt. This is the highest measured snowpack in this corridor since 2018, and its implications are significant:

- **Positive**: High glacier mass balance and abundant water for lower valley systems
- **Challenging**: Extended snow coverage on classic approach trails well into June; cornice formation persisting on north-facing couloirs and ridgelines
- **Route timing**: Classic summer routes accessible 3–5 weeks later than the 2020–2024 average

The MeteoSwiss mountain weather model has the snowline consolidating above 2,800m by early July, with most standard routes approaching normal summer conditions by mid-July.

## Mont Blanc Massif: Current Status

**Cosmiques Arête (Mont Blanc du Tacul)**: Currently in optimal condition. The bergschrund below the arête is well-bridged, and the snow quality on the northeast face is consistent. The Cosmiques Hut is open and operating at full capacity — book well in advance.

**Frendo Spur**: Technical sections above the Mer de Glace are holding winter snow, making this a more committing objective than typical for early July. Parties should anticipate 60° névé on the upper third.

**Tour du Mont Blanc (TMB)**: The popular circuit's high passes — notably the Col de la Seigne, Grand Col Ferret, and Col du Bonhomme — are snow-covered through early July with firm morning conditions. Microspikes are essential; an ice axe is advisable for early starts on the Ferret and Bonhomme.

## Bernese Oberland: Hut Access

The Swiss Alpine Club's (SAC) hut network opened on schedule, but several approach trails remain snow-covered:

**Konkordiahütte**: Accessible by the standard summer route, but the final 200m snow slope requires careful footwork and is recommended for parties carrying crampons. The hut guardian reports the glacier surface is rougher than recent years due to heavy winter accumulation settling unevenly.

**Hollandiahütte**: The route from Lötschental is clear below 2,400m; above that, expect consistent snow. Parties approaching from the Aletsch Glacier should note increased crevasse activity in the lower Grosser Aletsch following the warm May period.

**Rottalhütte (Jungfraujoch approach)**: Currently inaccessible by the standard summer trail due to a cornice collapse on the approach ridge in late May. SAC has marked an alternate route 200m to the west — look for red markers from the Jungfraujoch saddle.

## Glacial Hazard Advisory

The accelerating glacial retreat across the Western Alps is producing dynamic hazard conditions that require attention from all parties regardless of experience level:

**Crevasse fields**: Several glaciers have produced new crevasse systems in sectors that were historically solid snow bridges. The upper Glacier du Géant and the lower Gornergletscher are flagged as requiring careful current assessment before travel.

**Sérac zones**: Warm temperatures in May accelerated activity in known sérac fall zones on the Grandes Jorasses north face and the Matterhorn's Zmutt Ridge approach. Parties should minimize exposure time during the 10:00–15:00 melt window.

**Rockfall**: Permafrost degradation continues to produce rockfall from previously stable features. The Italian approach to the Gran Paradiso has seen multiple new fall lines this season — parties should move efficiently through exposed sections.

## Practical Planning Notes

- **Guides**: Given current conditions, engaging a certified IFMGA guide is strongly recommended for any mixed terrain objective above 3,500m
- **Emergency services**: Air Zermatt and Air Glaciers are fully operational; save the European emergency number (112) and local rescue contacts before departing the valley
- **Gear**: Full glacier travel kit (crampons, ice axe, rope, harness, crevasse rescue equipment) is required for all glacier approaches regardless of current snow coverage
- **Weather**: The Alps' notorious afternoon thunderstorm pattern typically establishes by mid-July; early starts are the primary safety tool available to any mountain traveler
